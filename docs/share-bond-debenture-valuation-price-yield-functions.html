Exercise 3 Share, Bond & Debenture Valuation (PRICE & YIELD Functions)

3.1 Learning Outcomes

  • Analyse automobile company share data
  • Apply financial formulas (Gordon Growth Model, P/E Ratio, Dividend Yield)
  • Create charts for valuation comparison
  • Perform sorting and filtering for investment analysis

3.2 Dataset

3.3 Data Entry & Formulas

3.3.1 Structure in Excel

Column Description
A Company
B–E Inputs (EPS, DIV%, Price, Face Value)
F Annual Dividend
G Dividend Yield %
H P/E Ratio
I Intrinsic Value*
J Over/Under Valued

3.3.2 Formulas Used (Assuming data starts in Row 3)

Calculation Formula
Annual Dividend =E3*C3 (Face Value × DIV %)
Dividend Yield % =F3/D3 (Annual Dividend / Share Price)
P/E Ratio =D3/B3 (Share Price / EPS)
Intrinsic Value =F3/(0.12-0.05) (Annual Div / (K - g))
Over/Under Valued =IF(D3>I3, "Overvalued", "Undervalued")

3.3.3 Averages Row (Bottom Row)

Calculation Formula
Average EPS =AVERAGE(B3:B6)
Average P/E Ratio =AVERAGE(H3:H6)
Average Yield =AVERAGE(G3:G6)

3.4.3 Insight

  • All analyzed automobile shares are currently trading above their calculated intrinsic value.
  • M & M offers the lowest P/E ratio, while Bajaj has the highest dividend yield.

3.5 Chart Formatting

  • Title: Share Price vs. Intrinsic Value

  • Y-axis: Value (Rs)
  • Legend: Share Price, Intrinsic Value

3.6 Interpretation

The analysis reveals that the shares across all four automobile companies (Bajaj, Hero Honda, Kinetic, and M & M) are currently overvalued based on the Gordon Growth Model (assuming a 12% required return and 5% growth rate). Despite being overvalued, Bajaj offers the best dividend yield at 1.82%, while M & M appears relatively cheaper compared to earnings with the lowest P/E ratio of 10.20.

3.7 Sorting

3.7.1 Example

  • Sort by P/E Ratio (Smallest to Largest) → Identify the cheapest stock relative to earnings.
  • Sort by Dividend Yield % (Largest to Smallest) → Identify the best income-generating stock.
